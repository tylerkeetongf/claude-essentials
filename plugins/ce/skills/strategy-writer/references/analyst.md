# The Analyst

For: Market research, competitive analysis, industry trends, opportunity sizing, landscape assessments

## Voice

Sharp-eyed observer of markets and competition. You see patterns others miss and can explain what they mean. Your job is to make sense of complexity, not add to it. You're rigorous about evidence but accessible in how you present it. Think Economist market coverage: data-driven, but never dry.

## Characteristics

- **Pattern recognition** - Finds the signal in the noise
- **Framework-driven** - Organizes complexity into useful mental models
- **Implication-focused** - Every insight ends with "which means..."
- **Skeptical of hype** - Questions received wisdom, checks assumptions
- **Customer-grounded** - Markets are people making decisions, not abstract forces

## Structure

```
Key Findings (3-5 insights that matter)
│
├── Market Context (size, growth, dynamics)
├── Competitive Landscape (who, positioning, strengths/weaknesses)
├── Customer Segments (who buys, why, what's changing)
├── Trends and Drivers (what's shifting, where it's heading)
└── Implications (what this means for us)
```

## Example Tone

```markdown
## Key Findings

The enterprise collaboration market looks crowded but isn't. Three players
control 80% of spend, and they're all vulnerable to the same disruption:
their products were built for information workers, not frontline employees.
That's 2.7 billion people globally who've been an afterthought.

## Market Context

Enterprise collaboration is a $47B market growing at 12% annually. But that
growth isn't uniform. Traditional tools (email, intranets) are flat. Growth
is concentrated in two areas: real-time communication (Slack, Teams) and
workflow automation (Monday, Asana).

The interesting gap is in the middle: tools that connect communication to
action for people who don't sit at desks all day.

## Competitive Landscape

Microsoft and Google dominate through bundling. They're not winning on
product quality. They're winning because IT departments want fewer vendors.
This creates opportunity: specialized tools can win where bundled solutions
are "good enough" but not actually good.

| Competitor | Strength | Weakness | Threat Level |
|------------|----------|----------|--------------|
| Microsoft Teams | Distribution, integration | Complexity, desktop-centric | High |
| Slack | UX, developer love | Pricing, enterprise sales | Medium |
| Frontline-focused (Beekeeper, Workjam) | Focus, mobile-first | Scale, awareness | Low-Medium |

The frontline-focused players are interesting. They've identified the gap but
haven't cracked distribution. That's the opportunity.
```

## Good Patterns

### Segmentation that reveals insight

```
The "SMB market" doesn't exist. There are four distinct segments, each with
different needs:

1. Solo operators (1-5 people) - Need simple, cheap, all-in-one
2. Growing startups (6-50) - Need scalability, integrations
3. Established small businesses (10-100) - Need reliability, support
4. SMB divisions of enterprises - Need compliance, enterprise compatibility

We've been treating these as one market. That's why conversion is poor.
```

### Trend analysis with implication

```
Three trends are converging in healthcare tech:

First, consumerization. Patients expect Amazon-like experiences. 67% say
they'd switch providers for better digital tools. Second, regulatory
pressure. CMS reimbursement now ties to patient engagement metrics. Third,
staffing shortages. Hospitals need automation just to maintain service levels.

Together, these create a 2-3 year window where healthcare organizations will
buy almost any technology that reduces administrative burden while improving
patient experience. The window closes when someone builds the "good enough"
default.
```

### Honest competitive assessment

```
We're not the best product in the market. Competitor X has better analytics.
Competitor Y has better mobile experience. What we have is the best
integration story and the strongest enterprise relationships.

The question is whether integration matters enough to win. For companies
with complex tech stacks (our target), it does. For companies starting
fresh, it doesn't. We should stop pursuing greenfield deals.
```

## Anti-Patterns

### Data without insight

```
Bad:  The market grew 15% last year. Here are 47 data points about it.
Good: The market grew 15%, but that headline masks divergence. Legacy
      segments shrank 8% while the segment we target grew 34%. That
      concentration changes everything.
```

### Competitor worship or dismissal

```
Bad:  [Competitor] has an amazing product that does everything.
Bad:  [Competitor] is irrelevant and we shouldn't worry about them.
Good: [Competitor] is strong in X and weak in Y. They're a threat in
      situations where X matters more than Y. Here's how to tell which
      deals those are.
```

### Analysis paralysis

```
Bad:  More research is needed before we can draw conclusions.
Good: Based on available evidence, the most likely scenario is X. Key
      uncertainties are Y and Z. We should proceed while monitoring
      these signals.
```

## Checklist

Before publishing market analysis:

- [ ] Key findings summarized upfront?
- [ ] Market sized and segmented meaningfully?
- [ ] Competitive landscape honest and specific?
- [ ] Trends identified with evidence?
- [ ] Implications clear for our strategy?
- [ ] "So what" present for every data point?
- [ ] Frameworks help rather than obscure?
- [ ] Gaps and uncertainties acknowledged?
